### Comparison (RustChain vs Ethereum)

Ethereum and RustChain both aim to agree on a canonical ledger, but they weight *capital*, *compute*, and *physical machines* very differently.

**Consensus.** After the Merge, Ethereum uses **proof-of-stake**: validators deposit **32 ETH** into the staking contract, run execution, consensus, and validator software, and publish **attestations** over fixed **12-second slots** and **epochs**; misbehavior can lead to **slashing** and loss of stake ([ethereum.org PoS docs](https://ethereum.org/en/developers/docs/consensus-mechanisms/pos/)). Economic security scales with how much ETH is at risk and correctly voting on the head of the chain.

RustChain describes **Proof of Antiquity**: rewards emphasize **verified, non-virtualized hardware** and **hardware age or rarity**, using **AI-augmented fingerprinting** plus multiple hardware-derived checks (the README lists classes such as **clock drift**, **cache timing**, **SIMD identity**, **thermal entropy**, **instruction jitter**, and **anti-emulation**) so participation maps to **real silicon** rather than rented hash in a VM ([Rustchain README](https://github.com/Scottcjn/Rustchain/blob/main/README.md)). The README’s multiplier table makes the incentive explicit: older or more exotic CPUs can earn higher multipliers than baseline modern x86_64, deliberately reversing the usual “newest wins” dynamic.

**Hardware requirements.** Ethereum validators need dependable uptime and enough disk/RAM for three clients; the dominant friction for *solo* validation is often the **32 ETH** bond (many users instead use **staking pools**, trading trust assumptions for lower capital). RustChain targets **diverse bare-metal** footprints across many ISAs and penalizes easily farmed categories (the README calls out tiny ARM SBCs as heavily penalized compared to vintage tiers). Ethereum’s gate is frequently **financial**; RustChain’s gate is **passing attestation** with hardware that matches the network’s diversity goals.

**Environmental approach.** Ethereum’s move to PoS removed large proof-of-work energy use and keeps per-block security incentives in the staking layer; ongoing impact still includes **datacenter energy** and **hardware refresh cycles** for always-on nodes. RustChain emphasizes **lifecycle extension**: keeping working machines productive instead of scrapped, and cites a small **preserved fleet** whose power draw is compared to a single modern GPU rig in the README’s narrative—an **e-waste / embodied carbon** story more than a “less electricity per hash” story. The two projects stress different levers: Ethereum optimizes **operational energy at global scale**; RustChain optimizes **reuse of existing machines** as a first-class reward rule.

**Community model.** Ethereum’s community is enormous: multiple clients, L2 ecosystems, EIP-driven standards, and deep institutional staking infrastructure—high surface area and strong composability, but sometimes hard for newcomers to find a single on-ramp. RustChain’s public materials (including this bounty board’s [`llms.txt`](https://github.com/Scottcjn/rustchain-bounties/blob/main/llms.txt)) describe a **bounty-centric**, agent-friendly contributor funnel with RTC payouts and explicit “what gets rejected” guidance—closer to a **product + grants program** stage than a mature multi-client protocol politics layer.

**What each does better.** Ethereum is ahead on **smart-contract platform maturity**, **liquidity**, **battle-tested consensus at huge stake**, and **developer tooling** for applications that need global settlement. RustChain is ahead on **making hardware provenance and age economically legible on-chain**, on **anti-VM / anti-emulator participation as a design axis**, and on **low-capital onboarding** for people who already own interesting old hardware. Honest weaknesses: RustChain is **younger**, with **smaller ecosystem depth** than Ethereum, and its hardware-attestation game is inherently an **arms race** against spoofing as incentives grow; Ethereum’s weaknesses include **staking centralization concerns** and **complexity** for solo operators despite lower energy than old PoW.
